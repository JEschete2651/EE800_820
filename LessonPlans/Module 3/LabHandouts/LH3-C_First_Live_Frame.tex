\labheader{3-C}{First Live Frame --- Systematic Integration Debugging}

\section{Objective}
Connect the STM32 interceptor (Threat) to the \FPGA{} and work through the four debugging layers (physical interface, baud rate and framing, protocol synchronization, data integrity) until the \mbox{ILA{}} captures the first \texttt{frame\_valid} pulse on live hardware. This handout is the central milestone of Module~3: a validated end-to-end data path spanning all four hardware components (cooperative target \MCU{} $\to$ \RF{} link $\to$ interceptor \MCU{} $\to$ \FPGA{} fabric).

\section{Prerequisites}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
	\item Lab Handout~3-A complete (all 9 checklist items passed, wiring plan documented).
	\item Lab Handout~3-B complete (debug bitstream programmed, \mbox{ILA{}} dashboard verified with 12 probes).
\end{itemize}

\begin{tcolorbox}[warning={Make Connections Now}]
	This is the first handout that physically connects boards together. Follow the wiring plan from LH3-A exactly. Common-ground first, then signal. Verify the voltage levels (3.3\,V on both sides) before applying power.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Procedure}

\subsection{Step 1 --- Physical connection}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
	\item With all boards \textbf{powered off}, run a GND jumper wire from the STM32 Threat Nucleo GND header pin to the Nexys A7 PMOD JA pin~5 (or pin~11, both are GND). This is the most important connection.
	\item Run a signal wire from the Threat's \texttt{USART3 TX} (\texttt{PC4}) to Nexys A7 \texttt{JA[0]} (Artix-7 ball \texttt{C17}). Verify the \texttt{.xdc} constraint from LH2-A/LH3-B names this port \texttt{uart\_rxd}.
	\item Power on the Nexys A7 first (so its \UART{} receiver is active before the STM32 starts transmitting).
	\item Power on the Threat Nucleo. Then power on both Target Nucleos (Target~A and Target~B for ping-pong traffic).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Step 2 --- Layer~1: Physical interface check}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
	\item In the Vivado \mbox{ILA} dashboard, arm the trigger on \texttt{uart\_rx\_pin} falling edge (start bit of any byte).
	\item Wait up to 5 seconds. The Threat transmits a forwarding frame every time it intercepts a packet (DATA + ACK pairs at $\sim$1\,s, so $\sim$2 frames per second; PAUSE bursts only when its user button is held).
	\item \textbf{If the trigger fires:} the physical layer is working. Proceed to Step~3.
	\item \textbf{If the trigger does not fire:} the signal is not reaching the \FPGA{}. Debug:
	\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em]
		\item Confirm the Threat's \texttt{USART3 TX} is on \texttt{PC4} (check the HAL initialization in the LH1-G firmware).
		\item Confirm the \texttt{.xdc} constraint file maps \texttt{uart\_rx\_pin} to \texttt{C17} and is active (not commented out).
		\item With a \mbox{DMM} in \mbox{DC} voltage mode, probe the signal wire at the \mbox{PMOD} connector while the STM32 is running. You should see a voltage between 1.5\,V and 3.3\,V (the \mbox{DMM} averages the \mbox{UART} idle/active transitions). A reading of exactly 0\,V means no signal or a broken wire.
	\end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Step 3 --- Layer~2: Baud rate and framing check}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
	\item Arm the trigger on \texttt{rx\_strobe} rising edge.
	\item When the trigger fires, examine the \texttt{rx\_data} waveform. The first byte of a forwarding frame should be \texttt{0x7E}. Check several captures to determine if bytes are decoding correctly or producing garbage.
	\item Monitor \texttt{fifo\_full}. If it asserts frequently, bytes are arriving faster than the parser can consume them --- this indicates a baud rate mismatch causing runaway byte generation. Compute the expected divisor: for a 100\,MHz \FPGA{} clock and 115200 baud, the divisor is $100{,}000{,}000 / 115{,}200 \approx 868$. Check the \VHDL{} \texttt{uart\_rx} generic.
	\item If \texttt{rx\_data} consistently decodes as garbage values (not valid frame bytes), the STM32 may be configured for a different baud rate or 2 stop bits while the \FPGA{} expects 1. Check the HAL \UART{} initialization in the Threat firmware.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{tcolorbox}[infobox={Baud Rate Deviation Tolerance}]
	The Module~2 \UART{} receiver uses $16\times$ oversampling and samples at the bit midpoint, which provides $\pm 3\%$ baud rate tolerance. The STM32L476 derives its \UART{} clock from the \mbox{APB1} bus; if the \mbox{PLL} configuration in the Threat firmware is different from what was used in LH1-G testing, the baud rate may be off. If \texttt{rx\_error} appears on every frame, measure the actual bit period with a logic analyzer or oscilloscope and compute the deviation:
	\[
		\text{Deviation (\%)} = \frac{|T_{\text{measured}} - 8.68\,\mu\text{s}|}{8.68\,\mu\text{s}} \times 100
	\]
	A deviation above 3\% requires adjusting the STM32 \mbox{APB1} prescaler or the \FPGA{} baud divisor.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Step 4 --- Layer~3: Protocol synchronization check}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
	\item With \texttt{rx\_strobe} triggering and bytes decoding correctly, the key observable is \texttt{frame\_valid} and \texttt{frame\_reject}. Set the \mbox{ILA} trigger on \texttt{rx\_strobe} rising edge and capture 50 consecutive \texttt{rx\_data} values. Scroll through the waveform and confirm \texttt{0x7E} appears as the first byte of each frame.
	\item If \texttt{0x7E} never appears in any capture, the Threat firmware is transmitting a different frame format than expected by the parser. Open the Threat debug \UART{} terminal simultaneously and watch one complete packet reception. The forwarding frame format (from Module~1 Table~6) should match what is in the \mbox{ILA} capture. If the Threat firmware was modified since LH1-G, it may have changed the start delimiter or frame structure.
	\item To confirm the full pipeline is working, change the trigger to \texttt{frame\_valid} rising edge and click \textbf{Run Trigger}. Arm and wait --- when it fires, \texttt{frame\_valid} will show a pulse in the waveform and \texttt{frame\_reject} should remain at 0. If \texttt{frame\_valid} never fires, scroll left in the waveform from the last \texttt{rx\_strobe} capture and check that the byte immediately after \texttt{0x7E} is \texttt{0x29} (the length byte for a 43-byte frame). If a different value appears at offset~1, the parser will reject the frame. Compare the captured byte stream against the forwarding frame definition in LH1-G.
\end{enumerate}

The \texttt{0x7E} start delimiter is visible at the beginning of the sequence, followed by the \texttt{0x29} length byte. \texttt{frame\_valid} asserts after the final byte is processed.}{fig:lh3c-fsm}

\subsection{Step 5 --- Layer~4: Data integrity (CRC) check}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
	\item If the parser reaches \texttt{CRC\_CHECK} but then asserts \texttt{frame\_reject} instead of \texttt{frame\_valid}, the \mbox{CRC} is failing.
	\item Set the \mbox{ILA} trigger on \texttt{frame\_reject} rising edge. Capture the complete 43-byte frame using \mbox{ILA} pre-trigger (set pre-trigger to 45 samples so the full frame precedes the reject event).
	\item Export the captured bytes from the \mbox{ILA} waveform viewer (\textbf{File $\to$ Export $\to$ ILA Data}) and manually compute \mbox{CRC}-8 over bytes 1--41 in Python:
	\begin{verbatim}
def crc8(data, poly=0x07, init=0x00):
    crc = init
    for b in data:
        crc ^= b
        for _ in range(8):
            crc = ((crc << 1) ^ poly) & 0xFF if (crc & 0x80) else (crc << 1) & 0xFF
    return crc

# Replace with your captured bytes:
frame_bytes = [0x01, 0x07, 0x0E, ...]   # bytes 1..41 of the forwarding frame
computed = crc8(frame_bytes)
captured_crc = 0xXX  # byte 42 from the ILA capture
print(f"Computed:  {computed:#04x}")
print(f"Captured:  {captured_crc:#04x}")
print(f"Match:     {computed == captured_crc}")
	\end{verbatim}
	\item If the computed \mbox{CRC} matches the captured byte~42 but the \FPGA{} still rejects, the \VHDL{} \mbox{CRC} engine has a bug (wrong polynomial, wrong initialization, wrong byte range). Re-run the Module~2 LH2-E testbench against this live frame as stimulus.
	\item If the computed \mbox{CRC} does \emph{not} match byte~42, the STM32 Threat firmware is computing \mbox{CRC} differently (different polynomial \texttt{0x31} vs \texttt{0x07}, different initialization value, or including/excluding the start delimiter). Compare against the Threat source from LH1-G.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{tcolorbox}[warning={CRC Polynomial Mismatch is the Most Common Layer-4 Failure}]
	The STM32L476 hardware \mbox{CRC} peripheral defaults to polynomial \texttt{0x04C11DB7} (CRC-32), not \mbox{CRC}-8. If the LH1-G firmware uses the hardware \mbox{CRC} unit without reconfiguring it, or uses a software \mbox{CRC}-8 with a different polynomial than \texttt{0x07}, the result will never match the Module~2 \VHDL{} \mbox{CRC} engine. Resolve by aligning both sides on polynomial \texttt{0x07}, initialization \texttt{0x00}, input/output no-reflect, covering bytes 1--41.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Step 6 --- First frame milestone}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
	\item Once all four layers are verified, arm the \mbox{ILA} trigger on \texttt{frame\_valid} rising edge.
	\item Before reading values, set \texttt{rssi\_out} to display as a signed decimal so it can be compared directly to the Threat's debug log. In the waveform viewer, right-click the \texttt{rssi\_out} row, select \textbf{Radix $\to$ Signed Decimal}. The value should appear as a negative number (e.g.\ $-45$) matching the \texttt{rssi\_dbm} field in the Threat's \UART{} output.
	\item When the trigger fires, examine the captured values for \texttt{beacon\_id}, \texttt{pkt\_type}, and \texttt{rssi\_out}.
	\item Simultaneously read the Threat's debug \UART{} terminal. The last reception line should show the same packet.
	\item Confirm the following match between the \mbox{ILA} capture and the STM32 debug log:
	\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em]
		\item \texttt{beacon\_id} equals the originating station's configured \mbox{ID} (\texttt{0x01} for Target~A DATA, \texttt{0x02} for Target~B ACK).
		\item \texttt{pkt\_type} matches the expected packet type (\texttt{00}=DATA, \texttt{01}=ACK, \texttt{10}=PAUSE).
		\item \texttt{rssi\_out} is within $\pm$2~dB of the \RSSI{} printed by the Threat.
		\item Both CRC flags pass: \texttt{frame\_valid} = 1 and \texttt{frame\_reject} = 0.
	\end{itemize}
	\item Optionally, hold the Threat's user button for 2\,s and re-arm the trigger. The Threat continues forwarding any DATA and ACK frames it receives during the button hold, so \texttt{frame\_valid} captures will still appear. Confirm the Threat's debug \UART{} shows \texttt{PAUSE -> ID=...} lines simultaneously, and that the targeted Target's \UART{} prints \texttt{"I'm Jammed"} --- this validates the jam path without requiring a separate frame type at the \FPGA{}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Step 7 --- Ten-frame field comparison}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
	\item Arm the \mbox{ILA} on \texttt{frame\_valid} and capture 10 consecutive frames covering both DATA (Beacon~ID \texttt{0x01}) and ACK (Beacon~ID \texttt{0x02}) ping-pong rows. For each, record \texttt{beacon\_id} (Beacon~ID), \texttt{pkt\_type} (packet type), and \texttt{rssi\_out} (\RSSI{}) from the \mbox{ILA} waveform.
	\item In the Threat terminal, capture the corresponding 10 reception log lines.
	\item Build a side-by-side comparison table:
	\begin{center}
		\begin{tabular}{c c c c c c}
			\toprule
			\textbf{Frame \#} & \textbf{FPGA ID} & \textbf{FPGA pkt\_type} & \textbf{Threat ID/type} & \textbf{FPGA RSSI} & \textbf{Threat RSSI} \\
			\midrule
			1 & & & & & \\
			2 & & & & & \\
			$\vdots$ & & & & & \\
			10 & & & & & \\
			\bottomrule
		\end{tabular}
	\end{center}
	\item All Beacon~\mbox{ID}s and \texttt{pkt\_type} values should match exactly. \mbox{RSSI} discrepancies up to $\pm$3~dB are acceptable (rounding in the 1-byte unsigned representation). Larger discrepancies indicate a field offset error in the parser or a byte-ordering issue in the \mbox{RSSI{}} extraction.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Verification Checklist}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
	\item[$\square$] Physical layer: \texttt{uart\_rx\_pin} transitions observed on \mbox{ILA} within 5 seconds of receiver power-on.
	\item[$\square$] Baud/framing: \texttt{rx\_data} decodes correct hex values; \texttt{fifo\_full} does not assert during normal traffic.
	\item[$\square$] Protocol sync: \texttt{0x7E} delimiter and \texttt{0x29} length byte confirmed in \texttt{rx\_data} capture; \texttt{frame\_valid} eventually asserts.
	\item[$\square$] Data integrity: \texttt{frame\_valid} fires; \texttt{frame\_reject} stays low on back-to-back frames.
	\item[$\square$] Ten-frame comparison: all \texttt{beacon\_id} and \texttt{pkt\_type} fields match Threat log; \RSSI{} within $\pm$3~dB.
\end{itemize}
